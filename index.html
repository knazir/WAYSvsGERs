The Evolution of General Education Requirements at Stanford University
In 2013, Stanford introduced a new general education requirement called the Ways of Thinking/Ways of Doing that replaced its predecessor, the GERs (General Education Requirements). The biggest change between the two systems is the flexibility of courses that students could take. GERs focused on expanding the breadth of courses taken by students while WAYS focuses more on developing a ‚Äúwell-rounded toolkit of intellectual skills‚Äù. We want to explore how the new WAYS requirements affect students‚Äô course planning and visualize the differences between WAYS and GERs. Specifically, we want to explore whether WAYS really did increase the flexibility of course choices for students and how the shift in requirements impacted enrollment for particular classes.
After having thoroughly examined the data, we decided to tell the story through 2 different perspectives: Course flexibility and enrollment.
Flexibility
We will first compare the flexibility between the GERs and WAYS. For each requirement, we will measure flexibility through the number of unique departments that make up a requirement as well as the number of courses within those departments.

We will compare both systems at their respective ‚Äúheights‚Äù (2012-2013 for GERs and 2017-2018 for WAYS). We noticed that after WAYS were introduced, the number of GER courses decreased and the number of WAYS courses increased over the next few years. Therefore, looking at these specific years paint a more accurate picture of the university‚Äôs ‚Äúideal‚Äù distribution for these general education systems.

Distribution of GERs

For the GER Area Requirements, students have to 1) take 5 Disciplinary Breadth (DB) courses, one from each DB category and 2) take 2 courses in 2 different Education for Citizenship (EC) subject areas. As we can see from the chart below, classes that satisfy the humanities requirement outnumber math and social sciences combined. The EC categories have the lowest number of courses offered; however, students only have to choose 2 classes out of the 4 categories.

Distribution of WAYS Requirements

The Ways program is comprised of eight Ways requirements. Students must take a total of eleven courses: 2 courses each in AII (Aesthetic and Interpretive Inquiry), SMA (Scientific Method and Analysis), and SI (Social Inquiry); 1 course each in AQR (Applied Quantitative Reasoning), CE (Creative Expression), ED (Engaging Diversity), ER (Ethical Reasoning), and FR (Formal Reasoning). Similar to the GERs, there is an uneven distribution of the number of classes across the different WAYS categories. Interestingly, students have to take 2 classes from the categories with the smallest number of courses, SI and SMA. ü§î

DB-Math has the second largest number of courses, but has the smallest number of departments. Departmental Makeup of WAYS Requirements

Compared to the DER donut charts above, WAYS requirements seem to come from a larger variety of departments. The biggest difference between GERs and WAYS is the addition of the Creative Expression requirements, which includes courses from the Theater and Performance Studies, Music, Dance, and Art Studio departments. Although the ethical reasoning requirement is the only requirement to have persisted across the two programs, it has evolved have a very different composition of departments. The GER EC-Ethical Reasoning had 104 classes across 19 departments, compared to WAYS-Ethical Reasoning, which includes 270 classes across 46 departments.

Comparison

Since there are more WAYS courses than GER courses and WAYS requirements are found in a larger number of departments, it looks like the WAYS system did increase the flexibility of course choices for students.

GER Makeup of WAYS Requirements

For all of the WAYS categories, the majority of classes are ‚ÄúNew‚Äù, which are classes that were not previously GERs. Again, this shows that CE was the largest difference between GERs and WAYS requirements.
