The Evolution of General Education Requirements at Stanford University
In 2013, Stanford introduced a new general education requirement called the Ways of Thinking/Ways of Doing that replaced its predecessor, the GERs (General Education Requirements). The biggest change between the two systems is the flexibility of courses that students could take. GERs focused on expanding the breadth of courses taken by students while WAYS focuses more on developing a ‚Äúwell-rounded toolkit of intellectual skills‚Äù. We want to explore how the new WAYS requirements affect students‚Äô course planning and visualize the differences between WAYS and GERs.

Specifically, we want to explore whether WAYS really did increase the flexibility of course choices for students and how the shift in requirements impacted enrollment for particular classes.
After having thoroughly examined the data, we decided to tell the story through 2 different perspectives: course flexibility and course enrollment.
Flexibility
We will first compare the flexibility between the GERs and WAYS. For each requirement, we will measure flexibility through the number of unique departments that make up a requirement as well as the number of courses within those departments.

We will compare both systems at their respective ‚Äúheights‚Äù (2012-2013 for GERs and 2017-2018 for WAYS). We noticed that after WAYS were introduced, the number of GER courses and WAYS courses gradually decreased increased respectively over the next few years. Therefore, looking at these specific years paint a more accurate picture of the university‚Äôs ‚Äúideal‚Äù distributions for these general education systems.

Distribution of GERs

For the GER Area Requirements, students have to (1) take 5 Disciplinary Breadth (DB) courses, one from each DB category and (2) take 2 courses in 2 different Education for Citizenship (EC) subject areas. As we can see from the chart below, classes that satisfy the humanities requirement outnumber math and social sciences combined. This is an interesting disparity as some could claim that humanities majors have fewer choices in their natural science or engineering requirements while others could conversely say that the system's flexibility is biased towards the humanities. The EC categories have the lowest number of courses offered; however, students only have to choose 2 classes out of the 4 categories.

Distribution of WAYS Requirements

The Ways program is comprised of 8 WAYS requirements. Students must take a total of 11 courses: 2 courses each in AII (Aesthetic and Interpretive Inquiry), SMA (Scientific Method and Analysis), and SI (Social Inquiry); 1 course each in AQR (Applied Quantitative Reasoning), CE (Creative Expression), ED (Engaging Diversity), ER (Ethical Reasoning), and FR (Formal Reasoning). Similar to the GERs, there is an uneven distribution of the number of classes across the different WAYS categories. Interestingly, students have to take 2 classes from the categories with the smallest number of courses, SI and SMA. ü§î However, all is not as it seems. While there appears to be bias towards what at first appear to be "humanities-related requirements," examining the department makeup of these requirements tells another story.

Departmental Makeup of GERs

How many unique departments make up the GER requirements? More departments allow students greater flexibility in their studies. DB-Humanities has the largest number of courses, and its courses are scattered across an enormous 56 departments. DB-Math has the second largest number of courses, but has the smallest number of departments at 11. The ‚ÄúOther‚Äù category includes all of the departments that make up less than 3.5% of the total number of courses. The question is, how does this compare to WAYS? Departmental Makeup of WAYS Requirements

Compared to the GER department makeups above, we can see that the WAYS requirements seem to come from a larger variety of departments. One of the biggest differences between GERs and WAYS is the addition of the Creative Expression requirements, which includes courses from the Theater and Performance Studies, Music, Dance, and Art Studio departments. Although the Ethical Reasoning requirement is the only requirement to have persisted across the two programs, it has evolved have a very different composition of departments. The GER EC-Ethical Reasoning had 104 classes across 19 departments, compared to WAYS-Ethical Reasoning, which includes 270 classes across 46 departments. Itt looks like WAYS is working towards increased flexibility as advertised.

Comparison

When comparing the two systems directly, the results are surprisingly clear. In terms of the average number of departments per requirement, average number of courses per requirement, and overall number of courses offered in general, WAYS beats GERs hands down. The increases are staggering, with WAYS seeing an enormous increase of 94.8% more courses per requirement on average. From these, it's clear that the WAYS system has worked towards offering students a larger variety in their choices by focusing less on specific areas of study and more on developing specific skills.

GER Makeup of WAYS Requirements

Another way that we can explain this increased flexibility is by examining how GERs translated into specific WAYS requirements. Below we have the makeup of each WAYS requirement in terms of which GER courses were also given WAYS requirements when the new system was introduced. What's surprising is that each requirement has courses from at least 4 different GERs, some like SI even go up to 9. Additionally, many WAYS requirements have a large portion of "New" classes that did not previously satisfy a GER requirement. Looking at it this way, it appears that WAYS has both increased the volume of classes that satisfy requirements while also mixing-and-matching between the original GER requirements to improve course flexibility.
Enrollment
Another metric we used to examine the effectiveness of the new WAYS systems was the course enrollment data. We have tracked significant changes in enrollment for two categories of classes:
  1. Classes that satisfied GERs but were not converted to satisfy any WAYS requirements
  2. Classes that satisfied no GERs but satisfy WAYS requirements.

GER classes that do not fulfill any WAYS requirements

Many classes were not given a WAYS requirement with the new system, Many of these experienced a non-trivial decrease in enrollment after the introduction of WAYS. Take CEE176B: Electric Power: Renewables and Efficiency for example. Originally, this class fulfilled the DB-EngrAppSci requirement, but its enrollment dropped by 33% within a year of the new system and over 50% by 2017. Enrollment data can give us insights into classes that students may have been taking only to satisfy requirements.

WAYS classes that did not fulfill a GER requirement

In this case, we're examining classes that did not originally satisfy GERs but do satisfy a WAYS requirement. Interestingly, between 2013-2016, the class with the largest increase in enrollment is MS&E193, which fulfills multiple WAYS requirements as well as a Technology in Society requirement for engineering students. Many CE and SI courses also experienced large increases in enrollment after the change from GERs to WAYS as these were classes that did not originally fulfill any requirements under the GERs. We could say that little-to-no increase in the number of classes that increased in enrollment after the introduction of WAYS is positive. This is because if enrollment doesn't spike, then we could assume that while GERs forced students to take classes in specific departments, WAYS allows students greater flexibility with more options to choose from
