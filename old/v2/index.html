The Evolution of General Education Requirements at Stanford University
In 2013, Stanford introduced a new general education requirement called the Ways of Thinking/Ways of Doing that replaced its predecessor, the GERs (General Education Requirements). The biggest change between the two systems is the flexibility of courses that students could take. GERs focused on expanding the breadth of courses taken by students while WAYS focuses more on developing a “well-rounded toolkit of intellectual skills”. We want to explore how the new WAYS requirements affect students’ course planning and visualize the differences between WAYS and GERs. Specifically, we want to explore whether WAYS really did increase the flexibility of course choices for students and how the shift in requirements impacted enrollment for particular classes.
Flexibility
We will first compare the flexibility between the GERs and WAYS system. For each requirement, we will measure flexibility by looking at the total number of courses that will fulfill that particular requirement and the departments that those courses belong to. We decided to use the courses from the 2013-2014 course catalog because that was the first year the WAYS system was implemented. Upperclassmen can decide if they want to stay on the GER system or switch to WAYS while freshmen will be required to fulfill WAYS requirements.
